## The pattern

Every system that rewards hardware
gets captured by whoever can afford the most hardware.

Bitcoin promised decentralization.
It ended with mining farms the size of warehouses.

Every system that rewards activity
gets captured by bots, agents, and phone farms.

Social media promised reach.
It ended with people hoarding devices
to manufacture views that were never real.

GPUs get more expensive. RAM gets more expensive.
Not because people need them —
because systems reward having them.

The woman in Lagos does not get closer.
She gets further away.

SUIJURIS has to know this before it exists.

## The first defense — contribution only counts when someone else needed it

A node cannot generate its own work.

You do not earn SUIJURIS by relaying.
You earn SUIJURIS by relaying something
that someone else asked to be relayed.

Demand must originate from a different node.
Delivery must be confirmed by the destination.
The carrier alone cannot sign its own receipt.

Three signatures for one contribution:

- **The requester** — I need this carried.
- **The carrier** — I carried it.
- **The destination** — It arrived.

No single node can complete the loop alone.
The contribution is verified by the event itself —
a real packet, a real need, a real arrival.

## The second defense — physics

Bots are cheap on the internet.
A thousand fake nodes cost nothing to spin up.

But Phantom's transmission layer runs on LoRa mesh —
physical radios in physical locations
carrying physical packets across physical distance.

Faking a relay means having an actual device
in an actual place carrying an actual packet
that another real node needed.

The attack gets expensive fast.
Multiple physical devices, in real locations,
relaying real traffic, with real destination nodes
confirming delivery.

At that point — you are just participating in the network.

Physics is the bottleneck. Not software.

## The third defense — reputation

A new node has no history.
It earns slowly until the network has seen it
contribute reliably over time.

A node that has been relaying real traffic for six months,
confirmed by dozens of independent nodes,
has weight that a fresh device does not.

You can buy a hundred devices.
You cannot buy six months of verified history
across a real mesh.

SUIJURIS earned is not flat.
It is multiplied by reputation.
A trusted node relaying one packet
earns more than ten fresh nodes relaying ten packets.

Quality of participation over quantity of hardware.

Reputation follows its own rules:

**It builds slowly.**
Time cannot be purchased.

**It comes from diverse sources.**
Confirmation from many independent nodes
matters more than volume with a few.

**It decays if the node disappears.**
You cannot build reputation and go idle
while it keeps earning.

**It is per node, not per person.**
The network does not know or care
if one person runs five nodes.
Each node stands on its own history.

The person with ten devices
gets ten slow-starting reputations.
The woman in Lagos with one phone
that has been a reliable node for a year
outearns all ten.

That is the Lagos Protocol applied to economics.

## The fourth defense — the ceiling

A node can only earn up to a maximum SUIJURIS per cycle,
regardless of how much it relays.

One phone running reliably at capacity
earns the same ceiling as a server rack.
Once you hit it, more work does not mean more reward.

Now buying ten phones gets you
what you could get with one.
The second device does not double your income.
It just spreads the same available work
across more hardware you paid for.

More hardware becomes wasteful, not profitable.

---

## The fifth defense — the shape of the network

If one node has a ceiling,
what stops someone from creating fifty nodes
on fifty identities?

Phantom will not do identity verification.
No KYC. No biometrics. No central registry.
That is a principle, not a shortcut.

But the network can see patterns
without knowing who you are.

**Geographic proximity.**
Fifty nodes all operating from the same physical location
on the same mesh network
look different from fifty nodes scattered across a city.
LoRa signals carry range data.
Nodes that are always suspiciously close to each other,
always relaying to each other,
always appearing and disappearing together —
that is a pattern the network can flag.

**Graph analysis.**
Real participation creates a messy, organic web
of interactions with many different nodes.
A farm creates clusters —
tight groups that mostly talk to themselves.
You do not need to know who owns them.
The shape of the connections tells you.

## The deepest defense — finite demand

This may be the real answer.

Real demand from real people is finite.

If there are only a hundred packets
that need relaying in your area,
a hundred fake nodes are competing
for the same limited pool of real work.

More nodes does not create more demand.
It just splits the same pie thinner.

You cannot manufacture need.
Someone has to actually want something carried,
stored, translated, processed.

The defense is not catching fake accounts.
It is making them pointless —
by tying rewards to real-world demand
that cannot be inflated.

Fifty nodes in a place where ten packets need relaying
means forty nodes earning nothing.
Not because they were caught.
Because there was nothing real to do.

## The five defenses together

| Defense | What it prevents |
|---------|-----------------|
| Demand-originated contribution | Nodes generating their own work |
| Physical transmission | Cheap bot swarms |
| Reputation over time | Fresh device farms |
| Per-node ceiling | Hardware hoarding |
| Finite real demand | Multi-account flooding |

No single defense is enough.
Together, they make exploitation
more expensive than participation.

That is the design.
Not a wall. A structure where honesty
is the rational choice.

## What we know now

The defense against capture is not one mechanism.
It is a structure of layered constraints
where each layer covers the weakness of the others.

Physics makes bots expensive.
Reputation makes fresh devices weak.
Ceilings make more hardware pointless.
Finite demand makes more accounts worthless.
And contribution that only counts when someone else needed it
makes the entire system rooted in reality —
not in what can be manufactured.

The woman in Lagos with one phone,
one reliable node, one year of history,
earning at the same ceiling as anyone —

she is not at a disadvantage.
She is the design.
